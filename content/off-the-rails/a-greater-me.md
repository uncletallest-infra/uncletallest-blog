Anything viewed through the perceptions of a human is inherently flawed.
Religion suffers from that more than most fields of thought — not because
the underlying questions aren't worth asking, but because the answers got
handed down through too many layers of human interpretation before they
reached you.

I've always steered away from the moniker of 'Atheist' or 'Agnostic' as
neither truly fit within my mind for what I could not articulate until
recently. Deist was more correct but still has issues for me since it
presupposes that the object of said Deism is a 'god'.

What I have decided is that I have finally managed to articulate what it
is that I 'believe' vis-a-vis a 'higher power'.

---

The 'higher power' is just a *greater me*. Each of us has one.

This simple statement fits with every honest faith out there in some way
that satisfies my self-imposed requirement for 'proof' before belief. It
even holds up when folks start talking about multiple dimensions and
alternate realities.

Since I was very young I have believed that *this* reality is merely a
plane of perception — a lab of sorts where our 'real' selves can get
answers to questions by living through them to find an answer. That has
not changed.

This Greater Me is exactly that: the primary shard of my being — soul,
if you must — and therefore has access to all my knowledge. The stuff I
can occasionally tap into seemingly without prior experience of it. They
have a question and need an answer. They send a piece of themselves to
this reality as an experiment to find the answers they seek. This life
and this reality are part of that journey and, at its end, will return
to their source.

---

This realization has resolved a lot of things I didn't even realize I
was still brooding over. It allows me to have faith in the one thing I
can prove exists — myself.

It also explains a few things about how I'm wired.

The brain that thinks in three-dimensional constructs, stores everything
as a future data point, refuses inherited mental models, builds the tools
when they don't exist — that's not a disorder. That's the experimental
apparatus the Greater Me sent here. The neurodivergence isn't incidental
to the experiment. It *is* the experiment.

If that sounds like a convenient rationalization, consider: I arrived at
this framework before I had language for the neurodivergence. The Greater
Me model came first. The diagnosis — or lack of one — came later. The
framework held.

This may sound silly to others. But it gives me hope for the future. And
it's the most honest answer I have to the question of why I am the way I
am, and why I keep building things anyway.

*— Pliny, the Moderately Informed*
